    How the figure is built

    1. The source data. Every attributed delay and cancellation on Britain's mainline railway, from Network Rail's Historic Delay Attribution dataset. Each row records the minutes lost, the train operator, and the cause (along with other info).
    2. Minutes lost per train. For every row, the delay and cancellation minutes attributed to it are added up. These are train minutes - the time the service itself ran late - and are summed separately for each operator.
    3. Turning train-minutes into human-hours. A late train costs time to everyone on board, so each train-minute is multiplied by an estimate of the people/passengers affected, then divided by 60 to convert to hours. The multiplier depends on the type of train:
      • Passenger trains use that operator's average passengers per train, worked out from the regulator's (ORR) published passenger-kilometres ÷ train-kilometres. This varies a lot by operator - the national average is about 126, ranging from roughly 50 on quieter regional services to over 250 on the busiest intercity routes.
      • Freight & engineering trains carry no passengers, so instead their delay is converted into equivalent passenger-hours using the Department for Transport's TAG value-of-time guidance - currently about 15 equivalent passengers per train. This keeps the real downstream cost of late freight (to the businesses that rely on it) in the total without treating a freight train like a full passenger service.
      Because both are expressed in the same unit (equivalent person-hours), they add together into the single headline figure.
    4. Grouping by cause. Each delay's incident-reason code is mapped to a category using Network Rail's official glossary, giving the top-10 breakdown.
    5. Time window. The headline covers a rolling ~12 month period. The toggle switch above re-scopes everything to all time, summing every period on record - currently … to ….

    Limitations

    • Derived passenger loadings are averages, not actual counts. Each operator's figure is an annual average. As a result, it doesn't reflect time of day, peak crowding, or direction of travel. A packed morning commuter train and a near-empty late-night one get the same multiplier, so individual trains are over or under-counted (though the mix of operators is properly reflected). This approach serves as a grounded guide, but isn't perfect - though I don't think it should be too far off.
    • The freight equivalent is the weakest assumption. Valuing a freight train's delay as a passenger-equivalent currently relies on national value-of-time figures rather than the specific cargo, so freight's share is a grounded approximation. If you can think of a better approach, please get in touch!
    • Cancellations are treated like delays. Cancellation minutes are folded in alongside delay minutes; in reality a cancellation affects a passenger differently (waiting for the next service, missing connections downstream, or abandoning the trip entirely), so combining them is a rough proxy.
    • Only attributed delays are counted. The dataset captures delays Network Rail has logged and attributed; some are recorded as uninvestigated or unattributed, and disruption outside that process isn't included.
    • The record has a fixed start. Anything before that isn't captured here.
    • Knock-on impact isn't fully captured. Missed connections, abandoned journeys and the wider knock-on to people's days fall outside these figures.
